    What are the common roles in a study group?

    What role could you play in a study group? Complete this survey to find out!
    • Logistics Coordinator
      • Determine best date for study session date and time.
      • Identify a location for the group to meet which fits both schedules and group needs.
      • Reserve study space (if necessary).
      • Establish next study session date and time (if necessary).
    • Agenda-Setter
      • Identify the purpose of the study group session through feedback with other group members (test/assignment specific, general review, social connection).
      • Distribute the agenda prior to the study group session.
      • A good agenda includes date & time, location, and purpose.
    • Timekeeper
      • Officially start and stop the study group session on time.
      • Determine a specific time for breaks.
      • A guideline for breaks by session length:
        • 1 hour or less session - no break
        • 2 hour session - one 15-minute break
        • 3 hour session - 2 fifteen-minute breaks
    • Questioner
      • Pose open-ended questions to spur discussion and challenge knowledge and understanding of the content.
      • Open-ended questions cannot be answered with a simple yes or no, for example:
        • Explain why...?
        • Explain how...?
        • What conclusion can be drawn from...?
        • What are the implications of...?
        • How are... and... similar or different?

    What are the different approaches to forming a study group?

    Informal Study Groups

    Easy! Get together with your notes

    • Students get together and either go through class material or review questions
    • Advantages: No real prep required; students can share ideas and knowledge
    • Disadvantages: May not be as productive; students may not be prepared to help one another or may give incorrect information

    What are the different approaches to forming a study group?

    Structured Study Groups

    Trickier! Let's talk more about how to do this.

    • Students decide what they would like to go over in advance so that they can prepare
    • The group may even assign tasks or parts of the material to each person to be the expert
    • Advantages: More productive; everyone is prepared and makes a contribution
    • Disadvantages: More effort required

    Study Strategies

    • Study Strategy #1 - Predicting Test/Exam Questions


      Have each person come up with study questions from class. You can also use a test or reviews questions in a textbook.
    • Study Strategy #2 - Summarizing


      Come up with a list of terms and concepts. Take turns having people summarize this in their own words. Have each person write down their definition, then read the actual definition.
    • Study Strategy #3 - Keeping it Fun


      Some ideas for games can include: matching terms and definitions, speed labelling, quizzing (or jeopardy), and finding interactive games online.
    • Study Strategy #4 - Using White-boards, Projectors, and Study Spaces


      You can use spaces and equipment on campus. Check out the Algonquin College blog to find study spaces on campus.
    • Study Strategy #5 - Coming up with Memory Tools to Share


      Figure out what group members are having trouble with remembering. Divide these concepts up and have each person come up with a memory tool, a song, a rhyme, to remember that idea. Share!

    The Planning Stage

    • Establish goals- what do people want to get out of the sessions? Who should be involved?
    • Determine how you would like to approach the material: teach or review the material or come up with study questions.
    • It can help to either assign someone the task of coordinating or get together to divide up the work
    • Ensure that everyone has something to do
    • Decide on a meeting time and place: think about where you will work best (avoid noise and distractions) and bring appropriate materials
